The Different Types of Computer Ports

Each computer is different, so the ports will vary from computer to computer. However, there are certain ones you can expect to find on most desktop computers and laptops. Learning how these ports are used will help whenever you need to connect peripherals to your computer, like a new printer, keyboard, or mouse (see Figure 17) [12].

The Universal Serial Bus (USB) Port

A USB port is a standard cable connection interface for computers and consumer electronics devices. USB ports can be used to connect almost any type of device, including mice, keyboards, printers, and digital cameras and to transfer digital data over USB cables. They can also supply electric power across the cable to devices that need it [20].

USB 3.1, also known as SuperSpeed+, is the latest version of the USB protocol. It is capable of transfer rates of up to 10 gigabits per second (Gbps), putting it in line with the first generation of Apple's Thunderbolt channel. USB 2.0, also known as hi-speed USB, can support a transfer rate of up to 480 megabits per second (Mbps) [12, 21].

Most desktop computers have two to four ports in the front and two to eight ports in the back of the system unit. A laptop computer has between one and four ports on the left, right, or both sides of the laptop (see Figure 18) [21].

The USB Type-C Port

USB Type-C is a new port for delivering data and power to and from computing devices. Because the USB-C port is symmetrical, a USB-C plug can be inserted either way, eliminating the frustrations of earlier USB ports and putting it on a par with Appleâ€™s reversible Lightning plug (see Figure 19) [22].

The Headset Port (or Jack)

A headset port (or jack) is used to easily connect speakers, microphones, and headsets without fumbling with the back of the computer [12]. Most desktop computers include in front of the system unit while laptops have it on the left or right side or on the front (see Figure 20) [12].

The Power Cable Socket (or Connector)

A power cable socket (or connector) is where you connect the power cord to the computer [12]. With the power cord in place, it can then be connected to a power outlet to provide power to the computer (see Figure 21) [23].

The Ethernet Port

The ethernet port is used to connect computers, servers, video game consoles, and other devices to the internet. It is typically located on the back of both computers and network devices and is used to establish a wired, as opposed to wireless, internet connection (see Figure 22) [24].

The Display Port

DisplayPort is a digital audio and video interface designed to deliver video and audio over a singular cable [25]. The port comes in two varieties: standard and the smaller Mini DisplayPort. Despite the difference in size, both ports transmit identical signals (see Figure 23) [26].

The HDMI Port

HDMI (high-definition multimedia interface) ports send audio, video and control information to computers, televisions, projectors, and audio/video receivers. Many computer monitors use this port along with an HDMI cable to transmit digital video and audio signals between the system unit and the monitor (see Figure 24) [27].

The DVI Port

DVI (Digital Visual Interface) is a common connection option used for connecting PCs to monitors. It is a standard for transferring video signals from DVI-equipped source devices (like PCs and DVD players) to a video display that also has a DVI video input connection (see Figure 25) [28].

Before HDMI was made available for home theater applications, DVI connections were used for transferring digital video signals from some source devices [28].

The Serial Port

A serial port is a serial communication physical interface through which information transfers, either in or out, one bit at a time (see Figure 26) [29]. Throughout most of the history of personal computers, data was transferred through serial ports to devices such as modems, terminals, and various peripherals [29].

The PS/2 Ports

The PS/2 (Personal System/2) port, also referred to as the mouse port or keyboard port, was developed by IBM. It is used to connect a computer mouse or keyboard to an IBM compatible computer. The PS/2 port is a mini-DIN plug containing six pins and is still sometimes found on all IBM compatible computers. (see Figure 27) [30].

The Different Types of Computer Slots

Computer slots are physical connections that allow the hardware of a computer to be expanded. In other words, computer slots enable users to upgrade their computers (see Figure 28) [31,32].

The SD Card Slot

An SD card slot is a slot that is used to insert a secure digital memory card into a computer, printer, or other device (see Figure 29) [33].

The Expansion Card Slots

An expansion card slot is a connection inside a computer on the motherboard or riser card. It provides an installation point for a hardware expansion card to be installed. For example, if your computer did not come with a video card, you could purchase one and install it here (see Figure 30) [12,34].
